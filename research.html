As a graduate student I studied how waves propagate through microstructures, for example how light (the wave) travels through a fiber optical cable (the microstructure). There are two possible approaches to this problem:

  1. Given a microstructure, what are all possible forms of waves that propagate through it? For example, many different frequecies of light go through glass, but very little will pass through a piece of wood.
  2. Say you want to send a particular frequency of light, and only that frequency, through a structure. The question then is what type of material should it be made from?

My work focused on the first problem. Figure 1 is a sketch of the type of structures that we considered (most definitely not to scale!). The different shadings represent different materials, so you can think of the center as a layering of sorts. The defining feature of this layered part is that it is small in scale as compared to the remainder of the structure and can be thought of as a manufacturing defect. Another way of saying this is that when you zoom out far enough, you no longer see these layers and (some would say that) neither does the wave of light which is propagating though it. So on average, these layers don't exits and the structure is made of just the one material.

That's the idea at least, but what we found is that this defect does in fact have a significant conribution to the properties of the waves that travel through this structure.

To build up intuition, consider first a structure without the defect and consider a laser beam shining on it as in the left side of Figure 2. The beam widens and decreases in intensity as it travels through the material. This is a similar effect to shinning a laser pointer directly on your finger tip to make your finger light up. The laser beam is no longer focused and spreads apart as it propagates through your finger.

In our research, we found that if we introduce a defect as in Figure 1, a new part of the propagating wave emerges - a localized part. The localized part does not change as it travels through the material and, in theory at least, would propagate unchanged forever. The right side of Figure 2 is a sketch of this behavior.

These types of unchanging waves are important because they can be used to send signals across long distances. They are the types of waves of light that travel through fiber optic cables. The materials that we worked with weren't cables, but they're still important and can be used in various small electrical components, such as cell phones or tablets.
