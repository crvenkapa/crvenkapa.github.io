As a graduate student I analytically studied how waves propagate through microstructures, for example how light (the wave) travels through a fiber optical cable (the microstructure). There are two possible approaches to this problem:

  1. Given a microstructure, what are all possible forms of waves that propagate through it?
  2. Given that you wish to propagate a particular form of wave, what type of microstructure would you need to design?

My work focused exclusively on the former problem. Figure 1 is a sketch of the type of structures that we considered (most definitely not to scale!). The different shadings represent different materials, so you can think of the center as a layering of sorts. The defining feature of this layered part is that it is small in scale as compared to the remainder of the structure and can be thought of as a manufacturing defect. Another way of saying this is that when you zoom out far enough, you no longer see these layers and (some would say that) neither does the wave of light which is propagating though it. So on average, these layers don't exits.

That's the idea at least, but what we found is that this defect does in fact have a significant conribution to the properties of the waves that propagate through this structure.

To build up intuition, consider first a structure without the defect and consider a laser beam shining on it as in the left side of Figure 2. The incident beam widens and decreases in intensity as it travels through the material. This is a similar effect to shinning a laser pointer directly on your finger tip to make your finger light up. The laser beam is no longer focused and spreads apart as it propagates through your finger.

In our research, we found that if we introduce a defect as in Figure 1, a new part of the propagating wave emerges - a localized part. The localized part does not change as it propagates through the material and, in theory at least, would propagate unchanged forever. The right side of Figure 2 is a sketch of this behavior. Waves of this form are used to transport signals through waveguides such as optical fibers.
