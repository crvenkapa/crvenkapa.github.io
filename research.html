As a graduate student I analytically studied how waves propagate through microstructures, for example how light (the wave) travels through a fiber optical cable (the microstructure). There are two possible approaches to this problem:

  1. Given a microstructure, what are all possible forms of waves that propagate through it?
  2. Given that you wish to propagate a particular form of wave, what type of microstructure would you need to design?

My work focused exclusively on the former problem. Figure 1 is a sketch of the type of structures that we considered. The different shadings represent different materials. The defining feature of the structures that we considered is the small scale layering of the different materials in the center of the structure. The scale is small in the sense that this part of the structure could be thought of as a manufacturing defect with respect to the remainder of the structure. On average, this section of the structure should not contribute to any significant properties. However, we find that this defect does in fact have a significant contribution.

To build up intuition, consider first a structure without the defect and consider a laser beam shining on it as in Figure 2. The incident beam widens and decreases in intensity as it propagated through the material. This is a similar effect to shinning a laser pointer directly on your finger tip. What happens? Your finger lights up. The laser pointer is no longer focused and spreads apart as it travels through your finger.

In our research, we found that if we introduce a defect as in Figure 1, a new part of the propagating wave emerges - a localized part. The licalized part does not change as it propagates through the material and, in theory at least, would propagate unchanged forever. Figure 3 is a sketch of this behavior.
